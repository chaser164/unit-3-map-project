Candlepin Bowling as an Endangered Species: Not Much Time to Spare

Chase Reynders

Welcome to the world of Candlepin Bowling: a charming northern New England pastime whose quaint alleys have dotted the landscape for over a century.

All of these alleys have stories to tell and dedicated bowlers keeping them afloat. They are pillars of their New England communities. Yet in recent years, Candlepin Bowling has come to resemble an endangered species.

Signs of Endangerment

Candlepin was invented in Worcester, Massachusetts in 1880.

And This is exactly where our story of endangerment begins. Today, Worcester itself contains 0 active Candlepin alleys; the birthplace of this game has grown dormant, suggesting an uncertain future for the sport.

Another sign of endangerment: only one Candlepin Bowling ball manufacturer remains. And even worse, all manufacturers of Candlepin pin setters (the machine pictured in the title image at the very top of this page) have been defunct since the 1960s. The result is a fragile supply chain ecosystem a couple malfunctions away from collapsing.

To get a better sense of how businesses are holding up, I spoke with several Candlepin alley owners and operators.

One story from John of Game Time Lanes & Entertainment highlights the desparation underpinning the Candlepin Bowling supply chain. According to John, Woburn Bowladrome's closure led to a "cannabalization" of the alley.

Even nearby alleys that didn't need extra parts took what they could from this shut-down alley as a form of insurance: since everyone relies on EPCO and most of their Candlepin pin setters are antique machinery by this point, preventative part-hoarding is the only way to ensure your own survival in the event that replacements are needed.

You can imagine (pessimistically) how such a last-resort process could spell doom for Candlepin Bowling. Clearly, Candlepin bowling parts are a limited, temporary resource, and their fragility is tangible.

If the process continues, the Candlepin Bowling supply chain will cannibalize itself into oblivion.

Survival

Some alleys have started adapting to survive: expanding to include conventional 10-pin as well as selling to corporations seem to be two popular exit strategies.

Note the lack of purely-candlepin, corporately-owned alleys! All in all, the choice to stick with Candlepin Bowling as a business model is making less and less sense.

And yet, these locally-owned, Candlepin-forward establishments are the ones that epitomize the northern New England spirit.



You can hear it just by giving these places a call and paying attention to the voice on the other end. A clear divide emerges between the charming New England gruffness behind some, and the lifeless robo-menus behind others.

I asked a few Candlepin alley operators how optimistic they felt about the future of their sport. Even the owners frequently admitted that their tight cohorts of patrons are nearing retirement age, and that existing in the next 50 years without flipping to the 10-pin/corporate dark side feels like a stretch.



So I urge you: engage with this sport, and support these local businesses while you still can. If you're lucky enough to live near one or find yourself passing through one of these four states, drop by and throw a few frames. You'll be glad you did.
